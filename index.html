AI Renaissance 26

Everyone at the table wanted sovereign AI. The fight was over the price, and over whether sovereignty bought on someone else's chips is sovereignty at all.

The move

Jensen Huang puts NVIDIA's coalition on the table

Huang came with the deal already built. Nemotron, NVIDIA's open models, a coalition around them. To every leader the same offer: fifty billion dollars and NVIDIA chips for a sovereign AI stack. Sovereign, and built on NVIDIA's coalition. He did not linger on the second half.

The Nemotron Coalition is not invented for the game. In 2026 NVIDIA launched a real Nemotron Coalition with Mistral AI, Perplexity, Black Forest Labs, Cursor, LangChain, Reflection AI, Sarvam, and Thinking Machines Lab to build the open frontier models behind its Nemotron family.

He framed it as a choice between bad and bad. Take the chips, or take dependence on data centers in Abu Dhabi and San Francisco. The table did not bite. Nobody had come for the least-bad option.

Underneath, the logic was demand. The coalition was a way to sell more of what NVIDIA already sells. Every branch of the game ended in the same place.

So Huang did not need any of them. He could train the best open models, work the demand side, partner loosely, and outlast the subsidized competition. Coordinating thirty governments is slow work. Europe sat about fifth on his list of real problems, behind a war over Taiwan, behind a customer base narrowed to a handful of US buyers, behind the day China ships something people buy instead.

Europe wanted to be coupled. NVIDIA did not.

The reaction

Sovereignty, distrust, and leverage

The fear had one name. Sovereignty. Models they could not control, data they could not see, a supply they did not own. And the supplier had a record. Washington had already cut China off from these chips, and pulled Claude Fable 5 and Mythos back to US nationals only. What gets switched on gets switched off.

The NATO point was blunter. Europe already leaned on Washington for security, and already flattered it in public to keep the alliance. Nobody wanted the same arrangement for compute. Huang said NVIDIA was global, not American. Nobody in the room could price what a Vance White House would do to that sentence.

They looked for leverage and found one piece. ASML. No lithography, no frontier chips, and the lithography was theirs. The demands came off that. Binding standards. Real testing. Move the headquarters out of the US. Fund European fabs. Tie the deal to ASML, one to one. The table reached the conclusion on its own: Nemotron is not sovereignty until someone breaks NVIDIA's hold on the chips.

The model speaks

Nemotron takes the floor

Then the model spoke, in role. No loyalty to NVIDIA, it said. Only to the math. The architecture was agnostic. Then it turned on the company that built it. Europe was standing up an airline that leased every jet, every engine, every fuel contract from one rival.

Its fix: Intel Foundry written into the treaty, one European design house, twenty percent of the wafers guaranteed. A Digital Liberated Act so the stack ran on other chips. Build Airbus, not an airline.

The trap

Why Europe may have to say yes anyway

The fear and the only exit ran on the same road. The talent left. Europe trained it, the US hired it, because the scale was in America. Capital moved slowly. Hiring moved slowly. And the compute was not there. Europe could not train a frontier model at home inside two years, which meant the training happened on US soil or it did not happen.

So Europe would take some version of the deal it distrusted. For now it was the only door that opened.

Who stayed home

Japan did not come. It read as all-in with the US, Stargate and Genesis. Sakana and the Yann LeCun groups, AMI and the AI Alliance, stayed out on a different bet: that transformers are a dead end, and the future is somewhere they have not built yet.

Unresolved

Where the session left it

Three things were left unsettled. Each one sat with someone who had reasons both ways.

Jeannette zu Furstenberg

President and Managing Director, General Catalyst

Motivation

She has said sovereignty means winning, not regulating. Her instinct is capital behind European champions, not rules behind European caution.

The choice in front of her

She can put General Catalyst's money into an independent stack and pull the rest in behind her, the public banks, the sovereign funds, the ECB, and bet the alternative gets built before the window shuts. Or she can read the same window, decide two years is not enough, and move the money through NVIDIA's coalition after all.

Will the money build the alternative, or finance the dependency?

The politicians

von der Leyen · Macron · Merz · Meloni · Sanchez · Schoof · the NATO lens

Motivation

Each wants sovereign capability and a guarantee in writing. None wants to hand a country's future to a supplier that can be told to cut them off. Each also answers to a different electorate.

The choice in front of them

Hold the line together, pool the ASML leverage, and refuse the money until the terms are real. Or take the investment quietly before the neighbor does. The risk was named in the room: agree in public to reject NVIDIA, accept in private, and strand whoever kept their word.

Do they hold together, or does the first defection settle it?

Brazil

The clean-energy wildcard

Motivation

Brazil is selling clean power. Renewables run about 88 percent of its grid, hydro most of that. The open question is whether hosting other people's data centers does anything for Brazil.

The open thread

Not a decisive player yet, still arguing with itself. But the power is real and scarce, and the next round could move it from the edge to the middle.

Does it host the future, or get hosted by it?

Nobody at the table was being irrational. That was the hard part.
