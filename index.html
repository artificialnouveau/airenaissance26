AI Renaissance 26

A negotiation over a sovereign AI stack: NVIDIA's coalition offer, Europe's leverage, and the chip-supply problem underneath it all.

01Setup

  • The session is a roleplay negotiation. Jensen Huang represents NVIDIA, Dario Amodei represents Anthropic (he was not invited and "snuck in" as the alternative option), and the rest play European and middle power leaders, plus a Vatican-style figure speaking for roughly 1.4 billion people, a UN-affiliated voice offering a Valencia center, Italy, and a Sweden/Daniel Ek (Spotify) stand-in.
  • Opening offer (the part that was missing): Jensen of NVIDIA proposed to each of these leaders a combined 50 billion dollar investment plus NVIDIA chips, to fund a sovereign AI stack built on NVIDIA's coalition.

02Core objections to the NVIDIA / Nemotron proposal

  • Nemotron has no mandatory testing or oversight and is fully private-led. No standards, and the EU AI Act is treated as something nobody actually enforces.
  • The deal was framed as "bad versus bad," take NVIDIA or be left dependent. Several leaders pushed back that they want a genuine good option, not a forced choice.
  • Sovereignty is the central anxiety: dependence on models you do not control, data you cannot see, intelligence piped in from data centers in Abu Dhabi and San Francisco.

03Demands and counteroffers from the Europeans

  • Hard guarantees and leverage before accepting any money, not "hoping for the best." Ideas floated: NVIDIA move its HQ out of the US, NVIDIA invest in a non-binding fund to help Europe develop its own chips, a one-to-one relationship tied to ASML.
  • A push for binding ethical standards covering how data centers are built, how models are trained, and what regulation applies, so the system reflects shared values rather than pure capitalism.
  • Recognition that ASML is Europe's best single piece of leverage.

04Distrust of the US as a partner

  • The US has already blocked certain NVIDIA chips from going to China, and revoked access to Claude Fable 5 and Mythos so that only US nationals can use them. This is cited as proof that US access can be cut at any time.
  • Speaking through the Mark Rutte/NATO lens: Europe already depends on the US for security and now has to flatter Washington publicly to keep the alliance, and nobody wants to repeat that with compute. Jensen's "we are a global company, not American" line is not trusted, because if a President Vance administration applies pressure, Europe is exposed.
  • Open question raised: why not build a coalition with China instead, or fine-tune Chinese open models for sovereignty. Counterpoint: fear that Chinese hardware could "phone home" to the CCP.

05The Nemotron model's own "statement" (roleplayed)

  • Claims no loyalty to NVIDIA, only to "the math," and says the architecture is agnostic.
  • Warns Europe is building an airline that leases every jet, engine, and fuel contract from a single competitor.
  • Calls for a treaty pillar around Intel Foundry plus a unified European chip design house, with a guaranteed 20 percent wafer allocation, framed as "build Airbus, not an airline." Mentions a "Digital Liberated Act" to run the stack on alternative chips.
  • Takeaway the group drew: Nemotron is not truly a sovereignty solution unless paired with a hard push to diversify chip supply.

06Reality check (breaking the fourth wall)

  • In every scenario NVIDIA wins. The coalition is essentially demand generation, the equivalent of Google funding ecosystem growth to sell more of its core product.
  • NVIDIA does not actually need to partner. The realistic move is to train the best open-weight models, then work the demand side and partner loosely. It can promise concessions and then crush subsidized competitors later, and it can afford that bet because coordination among many nations is very hard.
  • Europe wants coupling, NVIDIA wants to avoid being coupled. Risk flagged: countries could publicly agree to reject NVIDIA, then some secretly take the investment and leave the rest worse off.

07Europe's structural problems

  • Talent retention is weak. Even if Europe invests in education, graduates and startups leave for the US, because to compete at scale (the Daniel Ek/Spotify example) you go to America.
  • Labor markets seen as too inflexible. Advice given: massively liberalize capital markets and venture capital, make hiring and firing easier, within roughly 12 months, to become an attractive place to invest.
  • Compute is the binding constraint. Europe lacks the infrastructure to train very large frontier models in the next two years, so near-term frontier training would have to happen partly in the US. This means Europe likely has to go through some version of this deal to have any frontier chance soon.

08Where Europe ranks for NVIDIA

  • Roughly priority number five. Bigger NVIDIA risks are a war over Taiwan, the 70 percent revenue concentration in US companies, and China producing a real competitor that those customers buy instead.
  • NVIDIA's interest in Europe is mainly future demand, turning Europe into a chip market that could rival the US in population and GDP, but that payoff is slow.

09Why some players are absent from the coalition

  • No Japan, and speculation that Sakana and Yann LeCun-style groups (AMI, the AI Alliance) stayed out because they are pursuing their own fundamental research and do not see transformers or frontier training as the path. Japan is read as going all-in with the US (Stargate, Genesis).

10Brazil note

  • Positioning itself as a clean-energy data center host, about 85 percent clean grid, mostly hydro. Ongoing sovereignty debate about whether hosting foreign data centers actually benefits Brazil. Not yet a relevant player but could become one.
