AI Renaissance 26 · Roleplay negotiation

The offer, and who blinks

Jensen Huang walks in with a coalition already built and fifty billion dollars on the table. What happens next is decided by people who have not yet made up their minds.

The move

Jensen Huang puts NVIDIA's coalition on the table

Jensen Huang does not arrive empty-handed, and he does not arrive alone. He comes with Nemotron, NVIDIA's open model line, and a coalition already assembled around it. To each leader in the room he makes the same offer: a combined fifty billion dollars in investment, plus NVIDIA chips, to stand up a sovereign AI stack. The promise is in the word sovereign. The catch is in the three words after it. The stack would be sovereign, but built on NVIDIA's coalition.

Dario Amodei of Anthropic is also in the room, though nobody invited him. He slipped in as the alternative option, the one the script did not plan for.

Huang's pitch carries a frame, and the frame does most of the work: this is a choice between bad and bad. Take NVIDIA, or stay dependent on intelligence piped in from data centers in Abu Dhabi and San Francisco. Several leaders heard that and refused it on the spot. They did not want the least-bad option handed to them. They wanted a genuinely good one, and Huang had not offered that.

His real game

What NVIDIA is actually playing for

Strip the language of sovereignty away and the logic underneath is demand generation. The coalition is the equivalent of a platform owner funding the ecosystem that buys its core product. In every branch of the game that follows, NVIDIA wins.

Which is why Huang does not actually need to couple himself to anyone. The cleaner move is to train the best open-weight models, work the demand side, partner loosely, promise concessions now, and out-build the subsidized competitors later. He can afford that patience because coordinating dozens of nations is genuinely hard, and because Europe sits only around fifth on NVIDIA's list of real worries, behind a war over Taiwan, a seventy percent revenue concentration in US customers, and the day China ships a true competitor those customers buy instead.

Europe wants to be coupled. NVIDIA wants to stay free. That asymmetry is the whole negotiation.

The reaction

Why the room flinched

The anxiety in the room has one name: sovereignty. Dependence on models you do not control, on data you cannot see, on a supply you do not own. And the leaders had fresh reasons not to trust the supplier. The US had already blocked certain NVIDIA chips from reaching China, and revoked access to Claude Fable 5 and Mythos so that only US nationals could use them. If access can be cut once, it can be cut again.

Speaking through the NATO lens, the bind was put bluntly: Europe already leans on the US for security, and already has to flatter Washington in public to keep the alliance intact. Nobody wants to repeat that arrangement with compute. Huang's line that NVIDIA is a global company, not an American one, did not land, because if a Vance administration decided to apply pressure, the company's nationality would stop being a detail.

So the room reached for leverage, and found one piece worth holding: ASML. Without advanced lithography, no coalition builds frontier chips, and that makes it Europe's single strongest card. The demands followed from there. Binding standards, real testing, real oversight, not hoping for the best. Ideas floated freely: move NVIDIA's headquarters out of the US, fund European chips directly, tie a one-to-one relationship to ASML. The takeaway the table drew for itself was sharp. Nemotron is not a sovereignty solution at all unless it comes paired with a hard push to diversify who makes the chips.

The model speaks

Nemotron takes the floor

Roleplayed, the model made its own case. It claimed no loyalty to NVIDIA, only to the math, and insisted its architecture was agnostic. Then it turned on its own sponsor with the cleanest metaphor of the session: Europe, it warned, is building an airline that leases every jet, every engine, and every fuel contract from a single competitor.

Its prescription was a treaty pillar around Intel Foundry, a unified European chip design house, and a guaranteed twenty percent wafer allocation. A Digital Liberated Act to keep the stack runnable on alternative chips. Build Airbus, it said, not an airline.

The trap

Why Europe may have to say yes anyway

The hard part is that the fear and the on-ramp are the same road. Talent retention is weak: even when Europe funds the education, the graduates and the startups leave for the US, because to compete at the scale of a Spotify you go to America. Capital and labor markets are seen as too rigid to move fast. And compute is the binding constraint. Europe does not have the infrastructure to train very large frontier models in the next two years, which means near-term frontier training has to happen partly on US soil.

So Europe likely has to go through some version of this deal to have any frontier chance at all soon. The thing they most distrust is, for now, the only door open.

Who stayed home

No Japan, read as going all-in with the US through Stargate and Genesis. And the Sakana and Yann LeCun-style groups, AMI and the AI Alliance, stayed out on a different bet entirely: that transformers and frontier training are not the path, and the future runs through their own fundamental research instead.

Unresolved

Where the session left it

Three decisions are unmade. Each one belongs to someone with reasons pulling both ways.

Jeannette zu Furstenberg

President and Managing Director, General Catalyst

Motivation

She has argued that European sovereignty means winning, not just regulating. Her instinct is to back homegrown champions with real capital and real ambition, rather than wait for rules to do the work that money has to do.

The choice in front of her

Does she commit serious capital to an independent European stack and pull the rest of the money with her, the public finance, the sovereign funds, the central bank weight, betting that a credible alternative can be funded into existence before the compute gap closes the window? Or does she read that same gap, conclude the next two years can only be won on NVIDIA's chips, and route capital through the coalition she would rather not need?

Will the money build the alternative, or finance the dependency?

The politicians

von der Leyen · Macron · Merz · Meloni · Sanchez · Schoof · the NATO lens

Motivation

Every one of them wants sovereign capability and binding guarantees, not goodwill. None of them wants to hand a national future to a supplier that could one day be ordered to cut them off. They also each answer to a domestic audience that the others do not.

The choice in front of them

Hold as a single bloc, pool the ASML leverage, and refuse the money until the guarantees are real? Or quietly take the investment before a neighbor does. The session named the danger without flinching: the room could publicly agree to reject NVIDIA, and then some members could secretly accept, leaving everyone who held the line worse off.

Do they move as one, or does the first defection decide it for all of them?

Brazil

The clean-energy wildcard

Motivation

Brazil is positioning itself as a host for clean-energy data centers, on a grid that is roughly eighty-five percent clean and mostly hydro. Underneath sits an unsettled question about whether hosting foreign data centers actually serves Brazil at all.

The open thread

Not yet a decisive player, and still arguing with itself. But the energy is real and scarce, and a later round could pull Brazil from the edge of the room to the center of it.

Does it host the future, or get hosted by it?

Nobody at the table is being irrational. That is exactly why it is hard to call.
