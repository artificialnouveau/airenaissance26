AI Renaissance 26

How a country builds AI it actually owns: its own chips, compute, models, and the money to pool behind them.

The Premise

Given Europe2031The scenario this story plays against: a Europe that bets on specialized, lower-capital AI rather than the hyperscale frontier. See europe2031.ai., a near-future in which Europe has bet on specialized, lower-capital AI, this story is a theoretical next step. Call it Europe++: how Europe and the other middle powers might push past that and build frontier AI they actually own, stage by stage. The aim is a not-lose condition for them, with defense, sovereignty, economic competitiveness, culture, democracy, and rights kept intact.

Stage 0

NVIDIA Moves First

NVIDIA moves before anyone else, at the scale of tens of billions of dollars, by scaling its NemotronIn 2026 NVIDIA launched the Nemotron Coalition with Mistral AI, Perplexity, Black Forest Labs, Cursor, LangChain, Reflection AI, Sarvam, and Thinking Machines Lab to build the open frontier models behind its Nemotron family. Coalition into a nominally middle-power but largely American open-weight effort. The logic is direct: it sells more GPUs to every country that joins. NVIDIA organizes the supply chain, coordinates data and research groups across members, extends the coalition to more middle powers, and sells GPUs to national telcos on the back of the investment.

NVIDIA's own risks are not Europe. They are a war over Taiwan, a customer base concentrated in three US companies, and a Chinese competitor its customers might buy instead. That concentration is the clearest point of alignment with middle powers, who fear the same thing. Europe is mostly future demand, a chip market that could one day rival the US.

The entry price NVIDIA's own model named on the floor: a guaranteed twenty percent allocation of leading-edge wafer capacity, tied to European fabrication. Without it, the line went, you are not building Airbus, you are leasing the airline. Sovereignty begins at the transistor, not the tokenizer.

Stage 1

Public Capital and the Rules

Four or five governments decide whether to accept NVIDIA's offer and partner inside the Nemotron Coalition. In deciding, they set the rules for the open-model ecosystem. The output is simple: for each country, yes or no, and if yes, how much capital it commits.

The governments broadly like what NVIDIA is doing. The question is the terms, and what public money signals to private money.

Stage 1.5

Consortium Structure

A yes creates a problem. The countries now have reason to build a shared open-source foundation model, but no investor will put money into the Nemotron Coalition as it stands. It has to be wrapped in a new entity, structured to pull in European private capital.

Three decisions set the shape.

  1. Who holds sovereign control. One nation, a regional bloc, a public-institution group, or a public-private mix.
  2. How members break ties. Consensus, weighted voting, a lead member, or a rotating chair.
  3. The geopolitical posture. Aligned with a bloc, nonaligned, or selective bilateral deals. Posture decides whether the consortium can even buy the chips its product plans assume.

The defection risk sits here. Weighted voting with precommitted resources is the main defense against the US buying individual members out one at a time.

Stage 2

Private Capital

Europe's leading venture capital and the central banks decide whether now is the time to fund a frontier consortium. The pivotal call is General Catalyst's Jeannette zu FurstenbergPresident and Managing Director of General Catalyst and co-founder of La Famiglia. She argues that European sovereignty means backing homegrown AI champions with capital, not just regulating them.. If she commits at scale, the rest of the private capital follows. Mistral is already in; if NVIDIA and General Catalyst both say yes, the other European sovereign-AI firms, Aleph Alpha among them, commit too.

The table she sits at: Daniel Ek as a proxy for Europe's large angels, SoftBank for big Asian institutions, a Gulf fund, Norway's sovereign wealth fund, the European Investment Bank, Apple, ASML, and the European central banks. European institutional money rarely moves toward innovation without a public signal first, which is why Stage 1 comes before this one.

Stage 2.5

The Companies Commit

The CEOs of the leading AI companies in the participating countries decide how much to commit, and in what form: cash, compute, or in-kind supply-chain capacity. The outputs are concrete. A minimum capital target of roughly 100 billion dollars in 2026, 300 billion in 2027, and 500 billion in 2028, with a share of compute, capital, and in-kind contribution assigned to each country.

Compute is the constraint those numbers exist to close. Preferred data-center hosts are chosen for power: Norwegian hydro, French nuclear, Iberian solar. One scenario has a Gulf fund finance and build the data centers while Europe does the research.

Stage 3

Product and Business Strategy

With control and capital settled, the consortium decides what it actually builds and how it earns. The core options are mutually exclusive: one open foundation model built Airbus-style, a looser grassroots coalition, several models with fusion, a leapfrog onto world models, or applied AI on top of a Chinese base. Through Stages 1 and 2 the model stays open source.

This is the choice the earlier stages were paying for. A strategy the market rewards turns the committed capital, compute, and political cover into a product people switch to; the wrong one spends all of it on a model nobody adopts, and there is no second consortium to try again.

Two forces decide the market it lands in: how commodified frontier models become, and how capital-intensive they stay. The four outcomes are AI as a utility, Brent Crude AI, Specialized AI, and Big AI. Europe's structural strength is Big AI, where scale keeps paying and its industrial base and proprietary data matter. Stage 4

The Market Reacts

Last, the market answers. Governments, the public, and the enterprise buyers, Siemens and Bosch among them, decide what they adopt, across Europe, Asia, and the US. Their uptake is what tells the consortium whether it built something the world wanted, or another subsidized model nobody switched to.

In 2024 that uptake was still early and uneven. Across the EU, AI use is concentrated in the largest firms; most small and mid-size companies have not switched anything on yet. That gap is the market the consortium would be selling into.

Where the consortium reaches

The consortium's footprint: NVIDIA in the United States, with European states and their middle-power peers pooling behind a sovereign stack. Drag to spin the globe.

NVIDIA scales the Nemotron Coalition; the entry price its own model names is a guaranteed 20% European wafer allocation tied to European fabrication. Its real risk is not Europe but Taiwan, customer concentration, and a Chinese competitor: data center is 92% of revenue, ~61% from four customers.

Stage 1 — Public capital and the rules

Four or five governments decide yes or no and, if yes, how much capital. The decision sets the rules for the open-model ecosystem and signals private money.

Stage 1.5 — Consortium structure

Three decisions: who holds sovereign control; how members break ties (weighted voting with precommitted capital blocks bilateral buyouts); and geopolitical posture, which decides whether the consortium can even buy the chips its plans assume.

Stage 2 — Private capital

General Catalyst's Jeannette zu Furstenberg is the pivotal call: if she commits at scale, the rest follows. A ten-state coalition pooled reaches ~$18B, about a sixth of US capital but roughly 80% of its training compute.
