The Airbus for AI Story

As of early 2025, roughly 80% of the world’s consumer AI prompts ran through a single US lab (a share that has narrowed since). France’s flagship, Mistral, holds under 4% of its home market. The Airbus for AI proposal sketches what middle powers might build if they pooled their labs, compute, and people into one public AI company. The story below traces how that coalition would form, across twelve chapters and a preface, in cities on three continents.

Preface

The State of Global Technology Sovereignty

What we’ve imagined is a speculative future scenario that explores the weaponization of large technology players for political purposes that ultimately threaten the sovereignty of any nation reliant upon major US infrastructure.

Why? Problems naturally arise when a government does not control its own data, when a very small group of heavily-capitalized firms dominate access to tech infrastructure, the US and its potential ability to access data through the CLOUD Act and FISA, and there exists an ever-tightening relationship between these American-based tech companies and the current administration.

We want to stress the inevitability of a conflict in sovereignty when middle powers, like the EU, are dependent on access to infrastructure they don’t own. We want this to be a call to action as the first step towards a coalition built to establish a greater degree of authorship over their own decision-making. AI systems require an unusually high degree of coordination amongst pieces of a process that are tightly complementary. The central sin of the EU and other middle powers is the inability to coordinate across national interests to build genuinely powerful technology infrastructure.

The scale is worldwide, and so is the process of coalition-building. But if proper democratic sovereignty is to be assured, then we must think together about the interoperability standards of a new general purpose technology. We can’t continue to hide behind our borders and hope that we can chip away at the dependency problem alone. It’s time to get on the phone.

The Swedish VC has long been accustomed to “founder speak.” The practiced staccato of a personality that fits between the beginning and end of a slide deck, the nervous changes in vocal registers when any sort of dissonant question is asked, the silent remuneration that mimics thinking on an answer long decided.

The truth is, no matter how much money he might give to any one of these founders looking to accelerate their path to market, the first thing they’d need to do to have any chance of success would be to move to Silicon Valley. He had done it himself. The same pitching rounds, rejections, failures, practicing in the mirror to hit the right notes at the right time – all that comes from being an entrepreneur. Although he liked to remember with a bit of a smirk that raising money in 2008 was a much different proposition than in 2026.

He wasn’t hearing any pitches today. His worries had grown as his own company and fortune amassed. It wasn’t from an especially philanthropic part of his heart that this urge originated, but more so from a matter of Scandinavian common sense: how likely is it that Stockholm will remain the type of place he wants to continue to live in if all the founders and the builders and the technologists need to move to California? It was in this firmament of state-sanctioned computer access and ideological alignment that his own aspirations were built. He wasn’t so narrow minded as to think this was a unique case. In a dark room in his soul he harbored that same feeling that we all have, though none of us particularly like to admit to its existence, even as it provides a sustenance we can’t live without, that he was special, that circumstances could have never blocked his path. In Silicon Valley or in Stockholm.

But this was not a feeling he was proud of (none of us are), so he’d called an assembly of minds to plan through an approach to combat the magnetic pull of Silicon Valley capital and talent.

He had his Prima Materia cofounder and confidante, his Head of Science Translation, the former founder of Skype and now a VC, and the co-founder of Wise, one of the first Estonian billionaires, and now also a European-minded VC.

“The U.S. firms are leaving. I’m sure you all saw what happened with OpenAI’s European offices, but there are rumors of something bigger happening,” the initiator of the gathering began.

“Is this the time to make the regulatory push? I’m hearing from founders every single day that it’s impossible for them to scale if they stay in Europe. Maybe we doubledown. Jobs are at risk. The atmosphere is too heavy. It suffocates anything that tries to grow,” the Estonian responded.

“What I’m saying” the Swedish VC continued, “is that we haven’t been very good citizens of Europe. Of course, we all did what we had to do to make money so we could enjoy views like this. The Head of Science Translation passed around a piece of paper to each of the four men.

“What is this?” the Skype co-founder asked, looking down through his thickly framed glasses.

“A liferaft,” the paper-passer responded.

The Swedish VC stood up: “We cannot be caught flat-footed when the shoe finally drops and the Americans force us to choose between them or the Chinese. It’s a farce that it’s even come to this. What I’m proposing here is that we pool our collective capital and accelerate the development of Helsing into a multilateral defense company for the entire EU.”

“Your pet drone venture you mean?” the Estonian said.

“If we demonstrate a broad coalition of AI-first defense capabilities, it shows that we’re not relying on Department of War technology that we don’t even own. Public and private enterprise. For the good of the people and of industry. It starts with protection. Security. Fighting them at the source of their strength.”

They would raise money and then take Helsing public, stay in Europe, and make sure no one is hiding behind the walls of their own borders. When the day would finally come for an American blockade on technical infrastructure, Helsing would be there as evidence that key strategic capabilities could be built in house. It would soften the blow. But to the Swede, who had been bemoaning the regulatory atmosphere of the EU for decades, it would still need to hurt. He wanted to make his point. They will need to back us because they won’t have any other option. A transnational AI-first defense company. This buys them time. No one likes to look weak. And when the compute shutoff happens, everyone is going to look very weak. But Helsing could prove that the EU could bite back.

The room cleared and the Swede was left with his cofounders. They had thirty minutes before they courted their next round of suitors. Outside, the sea continued to rage while underneath it the data communication cables sat ferrying pulses back and forth between continents. You cut one of those cables and it’s an act of war. Such a thin, fragile thing. It’s easy to forget that the world exists perpetually on an axis between aggression and cooperation. That at some point the symbolism of words like “sovereignty” or “independence” meet their real world referent under a freezing sea where lasers propel data down fiber optic cables at the speed of light. Someone surely is trying to control the subsea infrastructure. But, looking at the undulating frozen wildness of the Baltic, one must assume a limit to what can be bought, controlled, and coerced without a response in kind.
